Research Report Deep Summary

1. Core Thesis: The Bottleneck in AI Data Centers Is Shifting from Compute to Connectivity

The thesis of this Bernstein May 2026 white paper is clear: AI data centers are no longer just a GPU, CPU, and HBM story. How data moves within racks, between racks, and across clusters is becoming the new performance bottleneck and the primary battleground for capital expenditure.

The report breaks AI data center connectivity into two categories:

Scale-up
Intra-rack Connectivity
Short-range, high-bandwidth interconnects — typical use case is short-distance connections between GPU/CPU/switch chips
Scale-out
Inter-cluster Networking
Network interconnects between racks and clusters — typical use cases include InfiniBand, Ethernet, optical modules, and switches
Key Judgment: Copper and optical will coexist, each occupying its niche based on distance, power, cost, and reliability. Near-term, copper remains dominant in scale-up; optical solutions will begin penetrating from the switch side of scale-out first.

CPO, or co-packaged optics, brings the optical engine closer to the XPU or switch chip. Its core value proposition is reducing signal loss, lowering power consumption, and improving bandwidth density and network reliability.

Yet the report is very measured on CPO timing: small-volume shipments will begin in H2 2026, primarily for scale-out; true mass volume is not expected until 2028. CPO for scale-up will come even later — potentially H2 2028 — as high-value XPU platforms will not readily accept unproven reliability risks.

Four key reasons:

1

Poor Serviceability

Traditional pluggable optical modules can be hot-swapped when they fail; a CPO failure may require replacing the entire switch or returning it to the factory.

2

Yield Challenges

Electronic ICs, photonic ICs, FAUs, micro-lenses, and fiber coupling all demand extremely high precision.

3

Complex Testing

Multi-stage testing is required at the wafer, die, and package levels.

4

Supply Chain Concentration

CPO supply chains are more concentrated than traditional optical modules, reducing CSP bargaining power.

3. TSMC COUPE: The Key Enabler of CPO Platformization

Bernstein believes TSMC's COUPE could become the dominant CPO platform. COUPE combines the EIC and PIC via hybrid bonding and precisely integrates micro-lenses and FAUs on top.

The report expects both NVIDIA and Broadcom to migrate to the TSMC COUPE path. TSMC's direct CPO/silicon photonics revenue contribution may be modest, but it will reinforce TSMC's competitive advantages in front-end wafers, SoIC, CoWoS, and advanced packaging ecosystems.

The primary CPO beneficiary chain includes:

  • Optical engine supply chain
  • CW laser
  • FAU fiber arrays
  • Test equipment
  • Advanced packaging platforms
  • Optical coupling and micro-optic components
Beneficiary names highlighted in the report include Chroma ATE, Lumentum, Coherent, TFC, Senko, FOCI, and ficonTec. Among covered companies with explicit ratings: Chroma ATE: Outperform, price target NT$1,660.

4. Copper Interconnects Will Not Exit Immediately — CPC Will Extend Their Lifecycle

The report's view on copper interconnects is important: copper will not be immediately displaced by CPO, but will continue through solutions such as CPC.

Traditional copper interconnects' advantages are low power, low cost, and high reliability; the disadvantage is that higher bandwidth means shorter transmission range. At 224Gbps, the report notes:

8. Investment Implications: Near-Term Boards & Materials, Mid-Term CPO, Long-Term Platform Ecosystems

The report's investment cadence can be distilled into three takeaways:

  • First, do not over-anticipate CPO fully displacing traditional optical modules or copper interconnects in 2026–2027. CPO is the direction, but mass adoption requires proven reliability, lower costs, and supply chain maturity.
  • Second, the more immediately visible opportunity is the increased value content in PCB/HDI/ABF/materials, driven specifically by the expanded midplane, backplane, and ABF substrate area and layer counts of Rubin and Rubin Ultra.
  • Third, the companies with genuine long-term moats are platform leaders and structural gatekeepers: TSMC in COUPE/CoWoS, NVIDIA/Broadcom in switching silicon and networking ecosystems, Ajinomoto in ABF film, Ibiden/Unimicron in high-end substrates, and Luxshare in copper connectivity upgrades.

Key risks are equally clear:

  • CPO reliability qualification falls short of expectations
  • CSPs continue to favor pluggable optical modules, delaying CPO adoption
  • PCB/HDI/ABF capacity additions in 2026–2027 are too aggressive, causing gross margin compression post-2027
  • Rising prices of T-glass, ABF film, and copper foil erode downstream profitability
  • Rubin/Rubin Ultra ramp timing changes
  • AI capex slowdown

AI Data Centers: The Real Bottleneck Has Shifted

Bottom Line Up Front: Near-term: boards and materials. Mid-term: copper connectivity upgrades. Long-term: CPO. Stop fixating on GPUs alone.
01

Connectivity Is Becoming the New Main Theme for AI Data Centers

This Bernstein May 2026 white paper has a very direct title:

Inside the War for AI Data Center Connectivity.

In plain terms: the AI data center connectivity battle.

Previously, when the market looked at AI hardware, the first things that came to mind were GPU, HBM, and advanced packaging.

But now the question is: as more GPUs are stacked and models grow larger, what happens when data can no longer move fast enough between GPUs, racks, and clusters?

That is the value of connectivity.

The report divides AI data center connectivity into two categories:

The first is scale-up: primarily intra-rack, short-range, high-bandwidth connections.

The second is scale-out: primarily inter-rack, inter-cluster, and network switching.

The core issue is not which one — copper or optical — wipes out the other.

The core is: copper and optical each occupy their own range, power, and cost niche.

Near-term, copper remains the dominant solution in scale-up.

Long-term, CPO and optical solutions will begin penetrating from the switch side of scale-out first.

Is the picture clear?

This is not a single-point technology substitution story — it is an entire supply chain value re-rating.

02

CPO Is Powerful, But Don't Expect a Full-Scale 2026 Breakout

What is CPO?

Simply put: placing the optical engine closer to the switch chip or XPU to reduce signal loss, lower power consumption, and improve bandwidth density.

Sounds impressive, right?

The data certainly is.

NVIDIA states that CPO switches vs. traditional pluggable optical modules deliver:

  • 3.5x improvement in power efficiency
  • 63x improvement in signal integrity
  • 10x improvement in network resiliency
  • 1.3x improvement in deployment speed

A traditional 1.6Tbps optical module consumes approximately 30W, of which DSP alone accounts for more than half.

Broadcom also reports that its CPO switch delivers 40% lower per-bit optics cost. Meta's testing of the Broadcom Bailly CPO switch demonstrated savings of over 500W in a 51.2T switch system.

So is CPO set for immediate liftoff?

Not yet.

Bernstein's assessment is measured: CPO will begin small-volume shipments in H2 2026, primarily for scale-out, to validate market response and supply chain readiness. True mass-volume shipments are not expected until 2028, per LightCounting.

Why the slow pace?

Because what hyperscalers fear most is not the power figures on a slide deck — it is real-world downtime in production data centers.

When a traditional optical module fails, you simply unplug and replace it.

When a CPO module fails, it may require replacing the entire switch, or even a factory return.

This is not a minor issue for CSPs.

Near-Term Strategy: CPO is the direction, but not a full-scale replacement in 2026. Investors who chase the "CPO" headline without regard for timing risk will likely be punished by the ramp schedule.
03

The Real Near-Term Certainty: PCB, HDI, ABF, and Materials

The most actionable insight in this report is not the CPO revolution narrative, but rather grounding AI connectivity upgrades in boards and materials.

That is the more realistic earnings story for 2026.

Bernstein's data is concrete:

  • 2025 global PCB market approximately $85 billion.
  • HDI market approximately $16 billion, representing ~18% of the PCB market.
  • In Blackwell racks, increased HDI usage means HDI+PCB value per GPU roughly doubles vs. Hopper, reaching ~$300.
  • Vera Rubin midplane layer count may increase from the mainstream 16+ layers to 40+ layers.
  • Rubin Ultra backplane may potentially reach 70+ layers.

What does this mean?

AI servers are not just about adding more GPUs.

They also require higher layer-count PCBs, more advanced HDI, larger ABF substrate footprints, lower-loss CCL, premium copper foil, and increasingly scarce T-glass.

In plain terms: AI connectivity upgrades have put boards center stage.

PCBs may have seemed like a mundane, traditional component.

But in AI servers, PCBs are no longer ordinary boards — they are the infrastructure layer for high-speed signal transmission.

Faster signals demand more expensive materials. Higher bandwidth demands more layers. Each platform upgrade pushes unit value higher.

This is why WUS, Victory Giant, Unimicron, Ibiden, Ajinomoto, and Nittobo keep appearing in every AI hardware conversation.

04

Upstream Materials: The Easier Choke Point

This section is the key point.

In the PCB value chain, genuine pricing power typically belongs not to the most publicized assembly layer, but to upstream materials.

Take ABF film.

Ajinomoto holds approximately 95% market share in ABF film — a near-monopoly position.

Its Functional Materials segment carries an operating margin of approximately 54%, contributing close to one-third of total business profit.

The AI end-use mix within ABF volume is estimated to increase from approximately 11% in FY3/24 to approximately 23% in FY3/27.

This is the classic "formerly a condiment company, now repriced by AI servers" thesis.

Now consider T-glass.

Nittobo is the key supplier, but new capacity will not come online until early 2027, meaning T-glass will remain tight throughout 2026.

This will directly pass through to pricing for substrate manufacturers such as Unimicron and Ibiden.

The report forecasts Unimicron's ABF substrate ASP increasing 5–7% per quarter in 2026, with AI-related revenue potentially representing ~50% of total revenue by 2026.

This is the most attractive position in the value chain:

Demand rising, materials tight, prices increasing, earnings with upside leverage.

Of course, capacity expansion will eventually arrive. Blind conviction is unwarranted. 2026 looks tight; post-2027, you need to monitor new capacity additions and depreciation pressures.
05

Copper Interconnects Are Not Dead — The Luxshare Angle Deserves Attention

The market tends to interpret CPO as "optical replacing copper."

But that is not the report's view.

Copper interconnects have very tangible advantages: low power, low cost, high reliability.

The challenge is that higher bandwidth means shorter viable range for copper.

At 224Gbps, passive DAC is typically limited to ~2 meters; AEC reaches ~7 meters. Beyond 10 meters, optical connectivity has a clear advantage.

But this does not mean copper is exiting imminently.

CPC will extend copper's lifecycle.

CPC directly integrates high-speed copper cable connectors into the ASIC or switch package, bypassing PCB trace losses and keeping 448Gbps-class copper links technically viable.

This is exactly the Luxshare thesis.

The report identifies Luxshare as a new copper connectivity supplier for GB300, with potential participation in the Vera Rubin platform, rated Outperform, price target CNY86.

In one sentence: CPO is the long-term horizon; copper connectivity is the current order flow. These two are not mutually exclusive.
06

How to Trade This Theme?

My read is that the AI connectivity theme can be viewed in three tiers.

1

Highest Conviction: PCB / HDI / ABF / Materials

Rubin and Rubin Ultra hardware upgrades will directly drive up layer count, surface area, material grades, and unit value of boards.

2

Medium Conviction: Copper Connectivity Upgrade

Copper will remain the dominant scale-up solution through 2026–2028. CPC will continue to extend the lifecycle, giving suppliers like Luxshare an opportunity to capture platform migration tailwinds.

3

Long-Term Trend: CPO

The CPO direction is very clear, but the cadence skews toward small-volume validation in H2 2026 and mass ramp in 2028. The right focus is on technology platforms, test equipment, lasers, FAUs, and advanced packaging — not chasing the concept blindly.

The strategy is straightforward:

For 2026 earnings realization, focus on PCB, HDI, ABF, T-glass, and copper connectivity.

For 2028 industry trajectory, focus on CPO, silicon photonics, test equipment, and the TSMC COUPE ecosystem.

But trading purely on sentiment, without regard for capacity addition timelines and valuation, is a recipe for getting hurt.

PCB/HDI in particular is a capex-intensive sector: 2026 may look very attractive, but post-2027 you need to watch new capacity additions, depreciation, and gross margin trajectories.

In one sentence: AI connectivity is not the next niche theme — it is the inevitable infrastructure pathway as AI shifts from stacking compute to competing on system efficiency. Those already positioned should continue monitoring earnings and order flow. Those not yet positioned should avoid chasing sentiment peaks and look for entry points where material tightness, platform transitions, and order fulfillment all converge.
